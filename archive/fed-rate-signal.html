Fed chair signals caution ahead of crucial next-quarter economic data release

In remarks that markets had been anticipating for weeks, the Federal Reserve chair indicated Thursday that policymakers would proceed carefully before making any adjustments to interest rates, citing persistent uncertainty in the global economic outlook.

By Daniel Reeves and Carolyn Hsu
TWT Business Correspondents
Updated 4:55 PM ET, Fri April 4, 2026

WASHINGTON — Federal Reserve Chair spoke before a gathering of economists Thursday morning and offered the clearest signal yet that the central bank is in no hurry to cut interest rates, even as inflation continues to cool toward its stated target. The remarks were parsed closely by investors, who had been hoping for a more definitive timeline on rate reductions.

The chair pointed to a series of second-quarter data releases — including employment figures, consumer price index readings, and retail sales numbers — as critical inputs that the policy-setting committee would need to evaluate before making any moves. "The data we have is encouraging, but it is not conclusive," the chair said. "Acting prematurely risks undoing progress that took considerable effort to achieve."

Markets responded with a brief dip following the remarks, as some traders had positioned for a more accommodative tone, before recovering to close modestly higher. The benchmark 10-year Treasury yield edged up slightly, while the dollar strengthened against a basket of major currencies. Equity markets across sectors ended the session with mixed results.

"Acting prematurely risks undoing progress that took considerable effort to achieve. We will let the data guide us."

— Federal Reserve Chair, remarks to economists

Several economists expressed frustration at what they characterized as the Fed's continued reluctance to signal a clearer path forward. "The uncertainty itself has a cost," said one fixed-income strategist at a major investment bank. "Businesses and consumers are holding back on long-term decisions because nobody knows when borrowing costs will come down." Mortgage rates, which remain near multi-year highs, have been a particular drag on the housing market.

The Fed's next scheduled policy meeting is six weeks away, and the chair declined to preview its outcome, emphasizing that decisions would be made based on incoming economic data rather than any preset course. Two Fed governors who spoke separately this week offered somewhat different readings of the economic landscape, underlining the genuine disagreement within the committee about the appropriate timing of any pivot.

Consumer spending data released earlier this week showed resilience despite elevated borrowing costs, adding to the case for patience. However, manufacturing activity contracted for a third consecutive month, and export orders declined, pointing to headwinds from softening global demand. Analysts say the combination of diverging signals makes the Fed's caution more understandable, even if it prolongs uncertainty for borrowers.
