Senate reaches landmark bipartisan deal on infrastructure and climate spending as debate enters final hours

Negotiators from both parties announced a breakthrough late Thursday, averting what many feared would be a prolonged legislative standoff that could have stalled key programs heading into the new fiscal year.

By Alex Moreno and Priya Anand-Cho
TWT Senior Political Correspondents
Updated 11:42 PM ET, Fri April 4, 2026

WASHINGTON — After more than 14 hours of closed-door negotiations, Senate leaders from both parties emerged Thursday night to announce they had reached a sweeping agreement on a combined infrastructure and climate investment package worth an estimated $940 billion over the next decade. The deal, if passed by both chambers, would represent the most significant domestic spending legislation in a generation.

The agreement covers a broad range of priorities that had previously divided the two parties: road and bridge repair, expanded broadband access in rural communities, upgrades to the national electrical grid, and a series of tax incentives aimed at accelerating the domestic clean energy manufacturing sector. Both sides described the final hours of talks as tense but ultimately productive.

"We did not agree on everything, and we never expected to," said the chamber's majority leader at a press conference just before midnight. "What we agreed on is that the American people cannot wait any longer for roads that work, for power that is reliable, and for a serious plan to keep our economy competitive in the next century."

"What we agreed on is that the American people cannot wait any longer for roads that work, for power that is reliable, and for a serious plan to keep our economy competitive."

— Senate Majority Leader, Thursday evening press conference

The minority leader, standing beside her counterpart at the podium, acknowledged that the final text represented significant compromises on both sides. Several provisions that each party had championed as non-negotiable were either scaled back or eliminated entirely during the final rounds of talks. Yet both leaders expressed confidence that the bill could command the 60 votes needed to overcome a procedural hurdle in the chamber.

The announcement came after a week of mounting pressure from governors, mayors, and business groups who warned that further delay would jeopardize hundreds of already-approved construction projects waiting on federal funding commitments. The National Association of Manufacturers issued a statement within minutes of the announcement calling the agreement "a foundation on which American industry can build."

Not everyone greeted the news with enthusiasm. Progressive lawmakers in the House indicated they would scrutinize the deal's climate provisions closely before committing their support, warning that the final package may have trimmed too many enforcement mechanisms meant to hold corporations accountable for emissions reductions.

Fiscal conservatives raised concerns about the spending levels even as they acknowledged the bipartisan nature of the agreement. A group of lawmakers issued a joint statement saying they would withhold judgment until the full legislative text and independent cost estimates were made public, a process expected to take several days.

Both chambers are expected to take up the measure within the next two weeks, though leadership has not yet committed to a specific floor vote date. The White House issued a brief statement calling the announcement "an encouraging step" but stopped short of an explicit endorsement, signaling that negotiations over specific provisions were likely to continue. Markets reacted positively in after-hours trading, with infrastructure-adjacent sectors posting notable gains.
