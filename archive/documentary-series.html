Award-winning director announces ambitious 10-part documentary series on post-war recovery

The filmmaker behind two of the most celebrated documentaries of the last decade revealed an expansive new project that will spend four years following communities in six countries as they rebuild from conflict — a scope critics are calling unprecedented in nonfiction filmmaking.

By Lena Castellanos and David Nkrumah
TWT Entertainment Correspondents
Updated 3:22 PM ET, Fri April 4, 2026

In a surprise announcement that sent ripples through the film world Thursday, the director released details of a decade-in-the-making documentary project that will follow families in six countries across four years, documenting the painstaking, intimate, and often overlooked process by which ordinary people rebuild their lives in the aftermath of armed conflict. The series, which secured co-production deals with three major streaming platforms, is expected to run for ten one-hour episodes with an additional theatrical release cut.

The project grew out of a trip the director took several years ago to document refugee camps for a short film that was never released. What she encountered — the tenacity, the grief, the unexpected humor and love she found amid devastation — compelled her to return, and then return again, until a short film became a feature and the feature became something far larger. "I kept thinking I was almost done," she said at a press conference in New York. "And then I would meet another family and realize I had only scratched the surface."

The series has already attracted considerable attention from the awards community, with one producer describing the footage assembled so far as among the most emotionally powerful documentary material he had ever encountered. Crew members who have seen early cuts described extended standing ovations at internal screenings.

"I kept thinking I was almost done. And then I would meet another family and realize I had only scratched the surface of what recovery actually looks like."

— Documentary filmmaker, New York press conference

The announcement comes as the documentary format is experiencing a creative renaissance. Streaming platforms, hungry for prestige content that generates conversation and awards recognition, have dramatically increased their investment in long-form nonfiction over the past three years. The new series represents one of the largest single documentary production budgets ever committed by a streaming consortium. Distribution in more than 140 countries is already confirmed.

Film critics who have followed the director's career say the new project represents both a natural evolution and a significant expansion of her established themes — the intersection of politics and private life, the costs borne by those furthest from the decisions that affect them most. "If this is even half as good as what she's capable of," wrote one prominent critic in a preview piece, "it will be required viewing."
