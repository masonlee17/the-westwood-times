UN envoy calls for immediate ceasefire as civilian death toll surpasses 4,000

The United Nations special envoy issued an urgent appeal Thursday demanding all parties to the conflict halt hostilities, warning that medical infrastructure is collapsing and relief organizations can no longer safely operate.

By Nadia Okonkwo and James Whitfield
TWT World Correspondents
Updated 6:31 PM ET, Fri April 4, 2026

GENEVA — The United Nations special envoy to the region issued an emergency plea Thursday calling on all warring factions to observe an immediate and unconditional ceasefire, citing a catastrophic deterioration in the humanitarian situation that has left civilians with no safe passage and medical facilities operating beyond their limits.

The death toll among non-combatants has now surpassed 4,000 since the latest escalation began six weeks ago, according to figures compiled by UN monitoring teams. The tally, which field workers caution is likely an undercount given restricted access to certain districts, has grown sharply in recent days as fighting has shifted into densely populated urban areas where distinguishing military targets from civilian infrastructure has become increasingly difficult.

"We are witnessing the systematic erosion of every protection that international humanitarian law is meant to provide," the envoy told reporters at a press briefing in Geneva. "Hospitals are being forced to close. Aid convoys cannot move. Families are trapped without food, water, or medicine. This must stop — not tomorrow, not at the end of a negotiating process, but now."

The Security Council held an emergency session Thursday afternoon but was unable to adopt a binding resolution, with two permanent members declining to support the draft text over language related to accountability mechanisms. A procedural vote to extend the envoy's mandate for an additional 90 days passed with 13 votes in favor. Diplomatic sources said negotiations over a compromise resolution would continue into the weekend.

Six major humanitarian organizations issued a joint statement Thursday warning they would be forced to suspend operations in the most affected districts within 72 hours if security conditions did not improve. One relief coordinator said three of her organization's local staff members had been killed in the past two weeks while attempting to reach isolated communities.

Regional governments have so far offered conflicting responses to the call for a ceasefire, with some endorsing the appeal and others insisting that military operations against what they describe as armed non-state actors must continue. International mediators are expected to travel to the region over the weekend to resume talks that collapsed last month.

Aid agencies are particularly alarmed by the condition of children caught in the conflict. UNICEF reported Thursday that at least 340 children have been confirmed killed, and that hundreds of thousands more have been displaced, with many separated from their families. Health officials warn of a potential outbreak of preventable diseases in overcrowded displacement camps that lack adequate sanitation.
