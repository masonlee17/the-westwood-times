UCLA's $425 million deficit casts a long shadow over student services, staffing, and the programs students rely on most

After the university's CFO was removed days after disclosing a near-billion-dollar two-year shortfall, students and staff are left asking what comes next for a campus already navigating federal funding threats and the rollback of equity programs.

By Marcus Washington, '25
7 min read
Updated March 30, 2026

In February, UCLA's then-Chief Financial Officer Stephen Agostini stood before campus administrators and described something most large public universities go to great lengths to avoid acknowledging: a structural financial crisis. The projected deficit for the current fiscal year, he said, stood at $425 million. When combined with the following year's anticipated shortfall, the combined gap approached $905 million.

Days later, Chancellor Julio Frenk removed Agostini from the CFO role. No official explanation for the removal was provided. The university said in a brief statement that leadership transitions in financial management were not uncommon and that a search for a permanent CFO would begin promptly.

That sequence of events, the disclosure followed almost immediately by the dismissal, left many on campus uncertain about what to believe and who to ask. Students who rely on tutoring centers, food pantries, mental health counseling, and retention programs want to know whether those services face cuts. Faculty who depend on research support want to know whether staff positions will be eliminated. Staff employees, many of whom have already witnessed layoffs in the wake of federal DEI pressure, want to know whether more are coming.

The university has offered few specifics. Frenk has acknowledged the deficit publicly and described a process of "strategic prioritization" that will unfold over the coming months, but has declined to identify which areas will bear the brunt of reductions. In a message to the campus community, he emphasized the university's commitment to its core academic mission and to the student experience, without defining what either commitment means in budgetary terms.

The deficit does not exist in isolation. UCLA is simultaneously facing the potential loss of nearly $600 million in federal research grants under the Trump administration's pressure campaign against DEI programs at major research universities. A federal court has blocked the immediate implementation of those funding threats, but the legal situation remains unresolved, and the uncertainty itself is affecting hiring and grant-dependent research programs.

Graduate students who work as teaching assistants and researchers ratified a new contract in March that includes wage increases of up to 45 percent, a significant improvement from existing pay scales that many had described as unsustainable in the Los Angeles market. That contract, while a meaningful gain for academic workers, adds to the university's financial obligations at precisely the moment when revenue is under pressure.

Student leaders have called on the administration to release a more detailed financial accounting and to include student representation in any planning process that affects student-facing programs. The USAC president, in a letter to Frenk sent last month, described the current level of information sharing as inadequate. As of publication, no formal response had been made public.

For students watching from the Powell Library study halls or the tables outside Kerckhoff Hall, the uncertainty is itself a kind of burden. "It's hard to focus on coursework when you don't know if the program that brought you here is going to exist next year," said one second-year student in the Graduate School of Education. "That's not a minor thing."
