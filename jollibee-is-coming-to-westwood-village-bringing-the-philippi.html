Jollibee Is Coming to Westwood Village, Bringing the Philippine Chain That Has Long Had a Devoted Following Among Filipino-American Students

The chain signed a lease on Weyburn Avenue in the space formerly occupied by Meet Fresh, adding to a stretch of Village dining that has seen more activity in the past year than at any point in recent memory.

TWT Staff
Updated Mon, April 6, 2026

Jollibee, the Philippine fast-food chain that operates more than 1,600 restaurants across 17 countries and has become a cultural institution for Filipino communities around the world, is coming to Westwood Village. The company signed a lease on a ground-floor space on Weyburn Avenue — the storefront most recently occupied by Meet Fresh, a Taiwanese dessert shop — with a targeted opening sometime in 2026, according to the Westwood Village Improvement Association. No official launch date has been announced, and the company still needs to secure permits and complete a build-out of the space.

For UCLA's Filipino-American community, the arrival carries a significance that goes beyond the practical convenience of a shorter drive. Jollibee has long functioned as a touchstone of Filipino identity and diaspora culture, its menu of Chickenjoy fried chicken, sweetened Filipino-style spaghetti, Yumburger, and Peach Mango Pie representing a particular culinary vernacular that Filipino families recognize from childhoods in Manila, Cebu, or immigrant neighborhoods across California. The chain's existing Los Angeles location on Beverly Boulevard has been a regular destination for UCLA students willing to make the trip. A Westwood location would eliminate that trip entirely.

Samahang Pilipino, UCLA's primary organization for Filipino and Filipino-American students, has over 200 active members and operates several community-facing programs including educational outreach and cultural advocacy initiatives. The organization's annual cultural night is among the largest student-produced performances on campus. The presence of a Jollibee within walking distance of campus will likely resonate well beyond that membership, given the broader size of UCLA's Filipino-American student population and the chain's well-documented ability to generate consistent foot traffic and long lines at its existing college-market locations.

Jollibee was founded in 1978 by Tony Tan Caktiong, who opened an ice cream parlor in Quezon City before expanding the concept into a full fast-food chain. When McDonald's entered the Philippine market in 1981, Jollibee responded not by mimicking the American chain but by doubling down on local flavors — sweeter sauces, a spiced fried chicken recipe tailored to Filipino preferences, and a menu architecture built around rice rather than fries. That strategy proved durable. Jollibee now outsells McDonald's in the Philippines and has expanded its North American footprint to more than 100 locations, with California historically accounting for the largest share of that presence.

The Weyburn Avenue location places Jollibee on the southern edge of the Village commercial corridor, a block from Broxton Avenue, where Raising Cane's has separately announced a late-2026 opening. The two announcements, arriving within months of each other, underscore the pace of dining investment the neighborhood has attracted this year. Bread Head and Sana'a Cafe are also in various stages of preparation, adding to a cluster of openings that the Westwood Village Improvement Association has described as part of the neighborhood's most significant commercial expansion in a decade.

Whether Jollibee can sustain the volume its brand is known for generating will depend in part on the same foot traffic and parking dynamics that every new Village tenant is navigating. But few incoming businesses arrive with as established a built-in audience as Jollibee does in a neighborhood adjacent to UCLA. For a chain that has always grown by opening near the communities it already serves, Westwood is close to an ideal fit.
